Collaborative Intelligence

Collaborative Intelligence characterizes multi-agent, distributed systems where each agent, which can be a human or a machine, autonomously contributes to a problem-solving network. This approach models problem-solving on evolution, where distributed, autonomous agents contribute and share control, rather than relying on a central, top-down authority.

Key Characteristics

Collaborative intelligence is often contrasted with the related concept of Collective Intelligence. The key differences are:

  • Participants: In collaborative intelligence, participants are not anonymous. Their unique identities, skills, and contributions are valued. In collective intelligence, contributors are often anonymous members of a crowd.
  • Control: Collaborative systems are decentralized and evolve based on contributions, much like an ecosystem. Collective systems often have a central controller who poses questions and processes responses.
  • Goal: The aim is to gather and value diverse, unique inputs to solve a complex problem. This differs from many collective intelligence applications that seek to find a "better-than-average" consensus or prediction from a large number of responses.

AI and Human Collaboration

The synergy between humans and Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a cornerstone of modern collaborative intelligence. When humans and smart machines work together, they can enhance each other's strengths, leading to significant performance improvements. The recent surge in open-source generative AI applications is a powerful demonstration of collaborative intelligence in action, showing how distributed, autonomous agents can share achievements and drive innovation in a bottom-up, evolutionary manner.

Applications and Examples

Collaborative intelligence can be applied in many domains. Some prominent examples include:

  • Wikipedia: The online encyclopedia is a prime example, where thousands of non-anonymous editors contribute and curate content in a distributed fashion.
  • Citizen Science: Projects that tap into the unique expertise and pattern-recognition skills of individual contributors. A well-known example is reCAPTCHA, which used human input to digitize books and improve spam detection.
  • Open-Source Software Development: Communities of developers from around the world collaborate on complex software projects, sharing code and expertise.
